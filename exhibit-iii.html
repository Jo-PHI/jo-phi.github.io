I

The Baker's Thumb:
What if AI Could See the Expertise
it is Trained to Overlook?

HashNode

Read an excerpt → Read in full at HashNode ↗

The baker rejects loaves that look fine. She presses her thumb into the crust. The yield — or the resistance — tells her something the sensors cannot.

Management reports fifteen percent waste. The quality control log shows a deviation. The recommendation: retrain the baker. What the log cannot show is thirty years of knowing. The slight resistance that tells her the crumb will be dense, the loaf will disappoint, the customer will not return.

What if AI could see that? Not the deviation — the intelligence inside the deviation.

II

Signal in the Noise:
When Human Deviation
is Missing Data

LinkedIn

Read an excerpt → Read in full at LinkedIn ↗

Every system that learns from data learns to see patterns. But what happens when the most important signal is the deviation from the pattern?

The delivery driver who slows down at the intersection three seconds before the light changes. The nurse who notices the patient is too quiet. The mechanic who hears something that isn't there yet.

These are not errors. They are the signal. And we are training AI to correct them.

III

Widening the Field of View:
Allowing AI to See
Human Intelligence

Prepared for arXiv  ·  Preprint

Read an excerpt → Request the full preprint ↗

AI systems are trained to recognise patterns. But the intelligence that matters most in human work is often the intelligent deviation — the expert who sees what the pattern misses.

This paper proposes PHI: Portraits of Human Intelligence — a framework for expanding the field of view of AI evaluation to include tacit knowledge, embodied expertise, and threshold recognition.

Tested with a single portrait, a language model shifted from recommending correction to asking whether the human might be detecting something the sensors were missing. Recognition improved substantially with minimal data.
